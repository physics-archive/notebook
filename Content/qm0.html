System Information in Quantum Mechanics

Quantum mechanics is an abrupt shift in interpretation from our classical models. In Schrödinger's model for particle mechanics, the function describing classical position $x(t)$ is replaced with a statistical object that we call the wavefunction $ \Psi(x,t)$.

  • In Classical Mechanics: a particle's dynamics are known if its position in time $x(t)$ is known. Solves a second-order ordinary differential equation.
  • In Schrödinger's Quantum Mechanics: information about a system is encoded instead in a probability wavefunction $\Psi(x,t)$. Solves a linear partial differential equation.

Motion in Newtonian Mechanics

To understand how $\Psi(x,t)$ provides observable information, we need to first explore how information is encoded in a classical system. In Newton's formulation, recall that a particle's position over time is the solution set $x(t)$ to the differential equation:

$$ \bbox[8px,border:1px solid black] {\vect{F}= \frac{d\vect{p}}{dt} = m \frac{d^2\vect{r}}{dt^2}} \tag{Eq. 1}$$

Eq. 1 - Newton's equation of motion states that acceleration is the second time-derivative of the position vector $x$. Assumes constant mass by treating it as a scalar.

Solutions to (Eq. 1) provide deterministic solution set for the system's motion over all time and space. Of course, for real systems our $F$ probably does not have an analytical form for us to make such complete deductions as we can in theory.

This ordinary differential equation (ODE) leads to our familiar relationships between $x(t)$ $v(t)$ and $a(t)$:

$$ \bbox[8px,border:1px solid black] {a(t) = \frac{d v(t)}{dt} = \frac{d^2 x(t)}{dt^2}}$$

Motion and Energy in Schrödinger's Quantum Mechanics

Schrödinger's analog to Newton's Equation is a complex-valued energy conservation equation:

$$ \bbox[8px,border:1px solid black]{ i \hbar \frac{\partial{\Psi(x,t)}}{\partial{t}} = - \frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{\partial^2 \Psi}{\partial x^2} + V(x,t) \Psi(x,t) } \tag{Eq. 2} $$

Eq. 2 - Schrödinger's Equation for the energy and motion of a particle. Partial differential equation whose solutions are functions of both position and time: $\Psi(x,t)$.

This is a linear, second-order, partial differential equation, with a similar characteristics to the wave equation. Obtaining analytical solutions to this PDE, for a given system potential $V$, is almost never trivial and often it is impossible.

Where is Classical Position Encoded in Schrödinger's Equation?

Undoubtably, as bizarre as (Eq. 2) is in comparison to the Newtonian definition for position, they must agree within classical limits of any system. If classical mechanics accurately predicts and explains behaviors for many [mainly macroscopic] systems, then those classical solutions should appear in our quantum-mechanical mathematics as well.

  • In other words, we need to verify that classical mechanics actually is an approximation for qunantum mechanics.

Schrödinger's PDE for energy conservation has solutions not of $x(t)$, but of a wavefunction $\Psi(x,t)$ which is related to $x(t)$ statistically (Gasiorowicz, pp. 46):

$$ \bbox[8px,border:1px solid black]{ \lt x(t) \gt = \int_{- \infty}^{+ \infty} \left[\Psi^* x\ \Psi \right]\ dx} \tag{Eq. 3}$$

Eq. 3 - Expectation value for position as a function of time. Here, $\Psi^*(x,t)$ denotes the complex conjugate of $\Psi(x,t)$.

In quantum mechanics, $ \hat{x}$ is an operator (i.e. $\hat{x}$ represents the operator for position). Operating on $\Psi$ is how observable behavior is predicted based on solutions sets to Schrödinger's equation.

  • The wavefunction is not directly observable, yet its probability density (|$\Psi^2$|) may be imaged with very modern technology.
  • Here, $\lt x(t) \gt$ is the probability of finding our particle between $- \infty$ and $+ \infty$ (entire x-axis). This boundary may be finite, and (Eq. 3) must be a convergent integral if it is to be valid (Gasiorowicz, pp. 46).
