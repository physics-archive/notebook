Classical Coupling

Page under heavy development. Not chronologically ordered at the moment (7/25).

In classical mechanics, systems must transfer energy through some medium in order to achieve coupling.

Why do we care about obtaining a mathematical model for a system of masses connected by a network of springs?

  • In classical electrodynamics, the "electron on a spring" model describes many systems (i.e. dielectrics), and in real materials these particles are numerous and interact with one-another.
  • In quantum mechanics, the harmonic potential is prevelant in (not limited to) molecular-energy approximations. Molecules consist of coupled particles, whose behaviors are oscillatory.
  • Coupling can also be undesired, such as parasitic electrical potentials or vibrations affecting a civil structure. We need to understand coupling if we want to minimize and address negative cases.

Recall that a particle on a spring is subject to a linear restorative force (Hooke's Law):

$$\blbox{F(x) = -kx} \tag{Eq. 1}$$ In terms of energy mechanics, the particle is subject the familiar harmonic potential $U(x)$: $$\blbox{U(x') = \int_{0}^{x} -kx'\ dx' = \frac{1}{2}kx^2} \tag{Eq. 2}$$ Therefore, our particle on a spring has has the total energy $E_{HO}$: $$\blbox{E_{HO} = \frac{1}{2}m v^2 + \frac{1}{2}kx^2} \tag{Eq. 3}$$
