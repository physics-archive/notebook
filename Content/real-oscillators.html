Oscillators in Nature

Oscillators are a natural phenomenon. Quantities that describe system motion may be cyclical (i.e. a rotating rigid body will experience cycles of position). Arguably of more importance: systems that are elusive to direct observation (i.e., effect of an electric-field propogating through some insulating material) may be accurately modeled as physical oscillators.

In fact, many nuclear and microscopic systems behave like damped oscillators. Even Maxwell's Equations form a wave equation, whose solutions are our electromagnetic wave-model of light (composed of oscillating E- and B-fields).

Often, it is easier to make mathematically identify the sources of change in our system, rather than obtaining an outright solution to how those changes actually result in our overall system behavior.

This is at the heart of why differential equations dominate our mathematical models for physical systems: describe how it changes, first - then use calculus to solve how that change effects system quantites (such as motion or energy).

Differential Equation for Undamped Harominc Motion

Hooke's law describes a restorative force: $F=-kx$. What is this $kx$ quantity really describing?

  • As our system moves away from equillibrium ($x_0=0$), a force directed at $x_0$ grows (directly proportional to $k$).
  • Notice that if our sign is reversed to say $F=+kx$, our force is directed away from equillibrium. Position will never return to $x=0$ once displacement away from $x_0$ occurs.
