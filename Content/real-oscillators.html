Oscillators and Diff. Eq. in Nature
Dimensional Analysis on $\beta, \gamma, k, m$

Oscillators are a natural phenomenon. Rotating bodies have cyclical motion, as does some mass on a spring. Moreover, systems that are elusive to direct observation (i.e., an electric-field propogating through some insulating material) are accurately accurately modeled as physical oscillators.

Many nuclear and microscopic systems behave like damped oscillators. Maxwell's Equations lead to a wave equation whose solutions are our electromagnetic model for light (oscillating E- and B-fields that propogate with a velocity).

Often, it is easier to mathematically identify the sources of change to our system, rather than arriving immediately at a mathematical model.

Constructing and Justifying our Differential Equation

Hooke's law describes a restorative force: $F=-kx$. What is $-kx$ really describing?

  • As our system moves away from equillibrium ($x_0=0$), a force directed towards $x_0$ grows in order to is restore system position; its direction always points towards the equillibrium point $x_0$.

Let us use intuition to define each contribution to $\vect{F}$ for our real mass-on-a-spring:

  • $F_{spring}(x)= -kx$: A restorative force that depends explicitly on position.
    Proportional to our spring constant $k$.
  • $F_{damp}(v)= -\beta \dot{x}$: A frictional force. Opposes velocity and causes energy loss (nonconservative force). Proportional to some damping factor $\beta$.

Newton's Equation for our spring system follows:

$$ F_{total} - F_{spring} - F_{damp} = 0$$ $$m \ddot{x} + \beta \dot{x} + kx = 0 $$ $$\blbox{m \frac{d^2 x}{dt^2} + \beta \frac{dx}{dt} + kx = 0} \tag{Eq. 1} $$

Eq. 1 - Linear, second-order, ordinary differential equation. Force assocaited with a mass on a spring, with friction accounted for in our second term.

Undoubtably, constructing (Eq. 1) was far easier than jumping straight to its solution, x(t). In solving (Eq. 1), we will demonstrate that x(t) is not trivial, and would not have been easily-obtainable with algebra or empirical methods alone.

  • Constructing differential equations is where much of our physics efforts get concentrated. A computer (or mathematician) can often do the brute work of actually solving the equations that we create to model our system. Refer to the Spherical Bessel Functions.
  • However, physical intuition is often required to simplify equations and make them solvable.

Solving our Differential Equation for Real Spring Motion

For completeness, it is necessary to consider x(t) to be complex-valued when solving our differential equation. However, our physical (observable) system is described only by its real part (not its imaginary). We will write Re[x(t)] after working out our complex solution. Going forward, i will play a fundamental role in defining oscillatory functions.

Making our lives easier: (Eq. 1) is actually homogonous in addition to being linear and ordinary. Given these properties, our complete (most general) solution needs to have the following properties:

  • Second-order, Linear diff. eq.: full solution must be a superposition of two linearly-independent solutions: $x_{full}(t)=C_1x_1(t)+C_2x_2(t)$. We will produce two arbitrary constants to solve for ($C_1,C_2$), given to us by initial conditions of our system.
  • Ordinary diff. eq..: usually far easier to appraoch and solve than partial differential equations. The solution set will be finite as opposed to infinite (in the case of PDEs).

Examine the most genearlized oscillatory function we can come up with, but label it as being a part of some index j to remind ourselves that we expect to find exactly two solutions of x(t) (j=2): $$x_n(t)=C_n e^{i \omega_n t} \tag {Eq. 2.1}$$

Eq. 2.1 - $C_n$ are complex-valued constants, and may have imaginary parts!

Our most generalized solution is some linear combination of these two solutions:

$$\blbox{x(t)=C_1 e^{i \omega_1 t} + C_2 e^{i \omega_2 t}} \tag{Eq. 2.2} $$

Substitute this full solution into our equation of motion to start determining our solution parameters ($C_n, \omega_n$):

$$ \frac{d^2}{dt^2} [C_n e^{i \omega_n t}] + \left(\frac{\beta}{m}\right) \frac{d}{dt} [C_n e^{i \omega_n t}] + \left(\frac{k}{m}\right) [C_n e^{i \omega_n t} ]= 0 $$ Perform our time-derivatives in terms one and two: $$ i^2 \omega_n^2 [e^{i \omega_n t}] + \left(\frac{\beta}{m}\right) i \omega_n[C_n e^{i \omega_n t}] + \left(\frac{k}{m}\right) [C_n e^{i \omega_n t} ]= 0 $$ Pull out common factors to reveal an interesting expression (recall $i^2=-1$): $$\blbox{C_n e^{i \omega_n t} \left( - \omega_n^2 + i\frac{\beta}{m}\omega_n + \frac{k}{m} \right) = 0} \tag {Eq. 3.1} $$

In proving that our equality holds, we need to eliminate this exponential term.

  • Algebraically, this is no problem: divide both sides by $C_n e^{i \omega_n t}$ to obtain our simplified eigenvalue equation.
  • A less hand-waivy argument: the quantity $e^{i \omega_n t}$ will never equal zero, so the only zeros (roots) in our quadratic equation (Eq. 3.1) have to come from particular values of $\omega_n$ (our eigenfrequencies):
$$\blbox{ - \omega_n^2 + i\frac{\beta}{m}\omega_n + \frac{k}{m} = 0 } \tag{Eq. 3.2}$$ Define the following substitutions (to simplify math and compress system parameters), and solve our auxillary equation for complex-valued eigenfrequencies: $$\begin{align} \beta /m \rightarrow 2\gamma \\ k/m \rightarrow \kappa^2 \end{align}$$ $$ - \omega_n^2 + 2i\gamma \omega_n + \kappa^2 = 0 $$ $$\blbox{\omega_{1,2} = i\gamma \pm \sqrt{\kappa^2 - \gamma^2}} \tag {Eq. 3.3}$$ Therefore, our general solution for damped spring motion is given by substituting (Eq. 3.3) in (Eq. 2.2): $$ x(t)= C_1 e^{i^2 \gamma t} e^{+i \left(\sqrt{\kappa^2 - \gamma^2}\right)t} + C_2 e^{i^2 \gamma t} e^{-i \left(\sqrt{\kappa^2 - \gamma^2}\right)t} $$ $$ \blbox{x(t)= e^{-\gamma t} \left(C_1 e^{+i \left(\sqrt{\kappa^2 - \gamma^2}\right)t} + C_2 e^{-i \left(\sqrt{\kappa^2 - \gamma^2}\right)t} \right) } \tag{Eq. 4}$$ We already see an interesting quality of our solution to damped motion: our particle is oscillating with some new frequency that is related by its natural frequency ($\kappa$ - the undamped frequency) and the systen damping (encoded in $\beta$). We can proceed to write down the purely-real (physical) form to this solution.
  • $e^{-\gamma t}$ - Factor corresponding to pure exponential decay over time. A damping term.
  • $\left(C_1e^{...} +C_2e^{...} \right)$ - Factor corresponding to undamped oscillation of the system in time.

Plot 1 - Plot of (Eq. 4) for arbitrary parameters ($\gamma \lt \kappa$). Predicts oscillating motion that decays in amplitude over time. Matches our expectation of how a damped harmonic oscillator will behave. Changes shape if $\gamma \ge \kappa$ (critical/overdamping).

The Real Part of our Solution

Our complex-valued solution is the most general description we have for damped oscillations in 1-D. However, the imaginary part (Im[x(t)]) does not describe any observable behavior, so we should disregard it and pursue a trigonometric form for (Eq. 4), so that we may readily apply our model to physical systems:

$$ x(t) \rightarrow \text{Re}[x(t)] $$ $$ C_n \rightarrow A_n+iB_n $$ $$ \sqrt{\kappa^2 - \gamma^2} \rightarrow \Omega$$

Note that $A_n, B_n$ are real-valued coefficients, and $\Omega$ is a frequency that we can think of as our damped oscillation frequency. Our real solution to motion comes out with surprising brevity:

$$x(t) \rightarrow e^{-\gamma t}\ \text{Re}\left[ (A_1 + iB_1) e^{+i \Omega t} + (A_2 + iB_2) e^{-i \Omega t} \right] $$ $$\begin{align} x(t) = e^{\gamma t}\ ( &\text{Re}[ A_1 \cos{t\Omega} + A_2 \cos{t\Omega} + B_1 \sin{t\Omega} - B_2 \sin{t\Omega}] \\ - &\text{Re}[ i A_1 \sin{t\Omega} - i A_2 \sin{t\Omega} - iB_1 \cos{t\Omega} - iB_2 \cos{t\Omega} ] ) \end{align} \tag{Eq. 5}$$

I have carefully grouped terms in (Eq. 5) such that it is clear which of our trigonometric terms are purely real versus purely imaginary -- our real solution for x(t) is clearly visible:

$$x(t) = e^{-\gamma t} \left[ (A_1 + A_2) \cos{\Omega t} + (B_1 - B_2) \sin{\Omega t} \right] $$ Compressing our solution constants yet again, we obtain an elegant solution to damped harmonic motion: $$\blbox{x(t) = e^{-\gamma t} \left[ A\cos{\Omega t} + B{\cos{\Omega t}} \right]} \tag {Eq. 6} $$
