Vectorizing Newton's Equation

One-dimensional quantities like $x(t)$ are useful in cases where we have symmetries to exploit, but we need to rewrite Newton's Equation using vector calculus if we want a true 3-dimensional model of motion.

  • Encoding our directional information in a position vector $\vect{r}(t)$ allows us to model motion directly in flat space $\Bbb{R}^3$.
  • Vectors often complicate our mathematical modeling, but we are rewarded with a clearer picture of energy conservation and motion in general.
  • This rigor will make the vector (and matrix) formulations of quantum mechanics far more approachable.

Rewriting Position, Velocity and Acceleration as 3-Vectors

In transitioning from $x(t) \rightarrow \vect{r}(t)$, The following will serve as a brief overview on vector-valued functions, and is best achieved by expressing our vectors in several equivalent forms.

The three elements of $\vect{r}$ correspond to motion in the respective directions:

$$\vect{r}(t) = \begin{bmatrix} x(t)\\ y(t)\\ z(t) \end{bmatrix} = x(t) \vecta{1}{0}{0} + y(t) \vecta{0}{1}{0} + z(t) \vecta{0}{0}{1} \tag{Eq. 1}$$

Since our particle - i.e. $\vect{r}$ - exist in flat space, the length of our vector (relative to the origin) is given by the Pythagorean addition of its components:

$$ |\vect{r}(t)| = x(t) \oplus y(t) \oplus z(t) = \sqrt{[x(t)]^2+[y(t)]^2+[z(t)]^2}$$

Differentiating $\vect{r}$ with respect to time will simply apply the scalaroperation to each component, leading definitions for $\vect{a}$ and $\vect{v}$ that are analogous to our one-dimensional model:

$$\vect{a}(t) = \frac{d \vect{v}}{dt} = \frac{d^2 \vect{r}}{dt^2} = \frac{d^2}{dt^2} \vecta{x(t)}{y(t)}{z(t)} = \vecta{\frac{d^2 x(t)}{dt^2}}{\frac{d^2 y(t)}{dt^2}}{\frac{d^2 z(t)}{dt^2}}\tag{Eq. 2} $$

Going forward, since time-derivatives behave as they did one-dimensionally, we can invoke the familiar the notation from Newton: $\frac{d}{dt}[f(t)] = \dot{f}(t)$

Putting everything back together, we obtain our vectorized Newton's Equation:

$$\bbox[8px,border:1px solid black]{\vect{F}(\vect{r},t) = m \ddot{\vect{r}} = \begin{bmatrix} F_x\\ F_y\\ F_z \end{bmatrix} = m \begin{bmatrix} \ddot{x}(t)\\ \ddot{y}(t)\\ \ddot{z}(t) \end{bmatrix}} \tag{Eq. 3}$$

Let's remind ourselves that force, on our lefthand-side, is determined by our system (i.e. whatever we're interested in), and $\ddot{\vect{r}}$ is the motion we're interested in solving.

Evidently, this vector formulation is providing simultaneous solutions for three separare differential equations. Our understanding of energy conservation (and conserved quantities in general) will be deepened by considering solutions to Newton's Equations in this matter.

Generalizing to N-Dimensional Functions

Notice, too, that this formulation is not limited to 3-dimensional vectors. For all flat coordinate systems (that is, $\Bbb{R}^k\ \text{for}\ k \in \Bbb{N}$), (Eq. 1) and (Eq. 2) can be generalized:

$$\vect{G} =\begin{bmatrix} x_1(t)\\ x_2(t)\\ x_3(t)\\ \vdots\\ x_j(t) \end{bmatrix}\ \text{and}\ \ | \vect{G}| = \sqrt{ \sum_{j=1}^{j} x_j(t)} \tag{Eq. 4}$$

Eq. 4 - Generalizations of (Eq. 1) and (Eq. 2), s.t. $\text{dim} \vect{G} = j$

Why would we even care about generalizing a system beyond the 3-dimensional coordiante system?

  • Dimensionality does not always have to denote spatial dimensionality.
  • Think of lists or arrays in programming: their dimension corresponds to their size, and sometimes this size may be quite large, dynamic, random, etc.
  • Even when approaching problems in our 3D space, generalized coordinates prove useful. In a system of N-many particles, we can denote different quantities $x_j$ for each particle, resulting in j-many dimensions to our system.
