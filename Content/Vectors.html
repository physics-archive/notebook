Vectorizing Newton's Equation

One-dimensional quantities like $x(t)$ are useful in cases where we have symmetries to exploit, but we need to rewrite Newton's Equation using vector calculus if we want a true 3-dimensional model of motion.

  • Encoding our directional information in a position vector $\vect{r}(t)$ allows us to model motion directly in flat space $\Bbb{R}^3$.
  • Vectors often complicate our mathematical modeling, but we are rewarded with a clearer picture of energy conservation and motion in general.
  • This rigor will make the vector (and matrix) formulations of quantum mechanics far more approachable.

Rewriting Position, Velocity and Acceleration as 3-Vectors

In transitioning from $x(t) \rightarrow \vect{r}(t)$, The following will serve as a brief overview on vector-valued functions, and is best achieved by expressing our vectors in several equivalent forms.

The three elements of $\vect{r}$ correspond to motion in the respective directions:

$$ \bbox[8px,border:0px solid black] {\vect{r}(t) = \hat{ \mathrm{ \boldsymbol{r}}} \mathrm{ \boldsymbol{r}}(t) = \begin{bmatrix} x(t)\\ y(t)\\ z(t) \end{bmatrix} = x(t) \vecta{1}{0}{0} + y(t) \vecta{0}{1}{0} + z(t) \vecta{0}{0}{1} } \tag{Eq. 1}$$

Eq. 1 - A vector in $\Bbb{R}^3$ measured from the origin (0,0). The unit vectors which encode direction are length 1, and are denoted with a hat. They are not necessarily constant in a given coordinate system.

Since our particle (and $\vect{r}$) exist in flat space, we can say that the length of our vector is a Pythagorean sum of its dimensional components:

$$\bbox[8px,border:0px solid black]{ |\vect{r}(t)| = x(t) \oplus y(t) \oplus z(t) = \sqrt{[x(t)]^2+[y(t)]^2+[z(t)]^2}} \tag{Eq. 2} $$

Eq. 2 - By definition, a unit vector is an operator which assigns direction to a quantity, but does not affect its magnitude: | $\hata{r}$ | = 1.

Differentiating $\vect{r}$ with respect to time will simply apply the scalaroperation to each component, leading definitions for $\vect{a}$ and $\vect{v}$ that are analogous to our one-dimensional model:

$$\bbox[8px,border:0px solid black]{\vect{a}(t) = \frac{d \vect{v}}{dt} = \frac{d^2 \vect{r}}{dt^2} = \frac{d^2}{dt^2} \vecta{x(t)}{y(t)}{z(t)} = \vecta{\frac{d^2 x(t)}{dt^2}}{\frac{d^2 y(t)}{dt^2}}{\frac{d^2 z(t)}{dt^2}} } \tag{Eq. 3} $$

Eq. 3 - Since time-derivatives are behaving as they did one-dimensionally (like a scalar operation), we can invoke the familiar the notation from Newton: $\frac{d}{dt}[f(t)] = \dot{f}(t)$

Putting everything back together, we obtain our vectorized Newton's Equation:

$$\bbox[8px,border:0px solid black]{\vect{F}(\vect{r},t) = m \ddot{\vect{r}} = \begin{bmatrix} F_x\\ F_y\\ F_z \end{bmatrix} = m \begin{bmatrix} \ddot{x}(t)\\ \ddot{y}(t)\\ \ddot{z}(t) \end{bmatrix}} \tag{Eq. 4}$$

Let's remind ourselves that force, on our lefthand-side, is determined by our system (i.e. whatever we're interested in), and $\ddot{\vect{r}}$ is the motion we're interested in solving.

Evidently, this vector formulation is providing simultaneous solutions for three separare differential equations. Our understanding of energy conservation (and conserved quantities in general) will be deepened by considering solutions to Newton's Equations in this matter.

Generalizing to N-Dimensional Functions

Notice, too, that this formulation is not limited to 3-dimensional vectors. For all flat coordinate systems (that is, $\Bbb{R}^k\ \text{for}\ k \in \Bbb{N}$), (Eq. 1) and (Eq. 2) can be generalized:

$$\vect{G} =\begin{bmatrix} x_1(t)\\ x_2(t)\\ x_3(t)\\ \vdots\\ x_k(t) \end{bmatrix}\ \text{and}\ \ | \vect{G}| = \sqrt{ \sum_{k=1}^{k} x_k(t)} \tag{Eq. 5}$$

Eq. 5 - Generalizations of (Eq. 1) and (Eq. 2), s.t. $\text{dim} \vect{G} = k$

Why would we even care about generalizing a system beyond the 3-dimensional coordiante system?

  • Dimensionality does not always have to denote spatial dimensionality.
  • Think of lists or arrays in programming: their dimension corresponds to their size, and sometimes this size may be quite large, dynamic, random, etc.
  • Even when approaching problems in our 3D space, generalized coordinates prove useful. In a system of N-many particles, we can denote different quantities $x_k$ for each particle, resulting in k-many dimensions to our system.

Obtaining Differential Elements for Position ($d \vect{r}$) and rewriting $m \vect{a}$

Let us start by defining a full differential element for $\vect{r}$ to describe arbitrarily small changes:

  • Newton's dot notation: let $\ddot{g}(t) \rightarrow \ddot{g}$. We do not want to keep redeclaring the time-dependence of our functions.
  • We should be thorough and apply the product rule to each component of $\vect{r}$ to prove that calculus operations behave analogously to our one-dimensional cases. That is, let us invoke the right-hand-side of (Eq. 1).
  • Recall that $\hata{x}$ is the unit vector for the x-direction. Unit vectors encode purely directional information.
$$\begin{align*} d \vect{r} &= d(\hata{x} x + \hata{y} y + \hata{z} z) \\ &= d(\hata{x} x) + d(\hata{y} y) + d(\hata{z} z) \\ &= \hata{x} dx + x \cancel{d \hata{x}} + \hata{y} dy \\ & \ + y \cancel{d \hata{y}} + \hata{z} dz + z \cancel{d \hata{z}} \\ d \vect{r} &= \bbox[8px,border:1px solid black]{ \hata{x} dx + \hata{y} dy + \hata{z} dz} \end{align*} \tag{Eq. 6}$$

Eq. 6 - Differentials of the Cartesian unit vectors are equal to 0, as these vectors (in Eq. 1) are constant. While this may seem obvious that differentiating $d \vect{r}$ produces our cartesian differentials neatly, other coordinate systems do not have such obvious differential elements.

Finally, let us invoke the chain rule to express $m \ddot{\vect{r}}$ as a relationship involving velocity, in order to match the quantities we care about when discussing energy relationship (think kinetic energy). For each component $j$ in $\vect{r_j}$

$$ \begin{align} m \ddot{x_j} &= m \frac{dv_j}{dt}\\ &=m \frac{dv_j}{dx_j} \\ &=m \frac{dv_j}{\cancel{dx_j}} \frac{\cancel{dx_j}}{dt}\\ m \ddot{x_j} &= \bbox[8px,border:1px solid black]{m v \frac{dv_j}{dx_j}} \end{align} \tag{Eq. 7} $$

Eq. 7 - This relationship between velocity, mass, and the derivative of velocity w.r.t. position is crucial for arriving at our conclusions for conserved quantities. Interpreting this in-between quantity is trickier than analyzing (Eq. 8), arguably.

Obtaining a Conservation Statement from Newton's Equation

Now we are ready to express Newton's Equation as a conservation statement. Multiplying our differential $d \vect{r}$ through (Eq. 1), we can resolve the conserved quantity $E$, the total system energy:

$$ \begin{align} \vect{F}(\vect{r}) \cdot d \vect{r} &= m \frac{d \vect{v}}{dx_j} \cdot d \vect{r}\\ \vecta{F_x}{F_y}{F_z}^T \vecta{dx}{dy}{dz} &=m \vecta{\frac{dv_x}{dx}}{\frac{dv_y}{dy}}{\frac{dv_z}{dz}}^T \vecta{dx}{dy}{dz} \\\\ F_x dx + F_y dy + F_z dz &= m (v_x dv_x + v_y dv_y + v_z dv_z) \end{align}$$
$$\begin{align} \blbox{\vect{F}(\vect{r}) \cdot d \vect{r} = m ( \vect{v} \cdot d \vect{v})} \end{align}$$

Integrate both sides of this expression w.r.t. the differential elements $d \vect{r}$ and $\ d \vect{v}$:

$$ \begin{align} \int_r \vect{F}(\vect{r}) \cdot d \vect{r} &= \int \vect{v} \cdot d \vect{v}\\ \int_{x_0}^{x} F_x\ dx + \int_{y_0}^{y} F_y\ dy + \int_{z_0}^{z} F_z\ dz &= \int_{v_{0}}^{v_x} v_x\ dv_x + \int_{v_0}^{v_y} v_y\ dv_y \ + \int_{v_0}^{v_z} v_z\ dv_z \end{align} \tag{Eq. 8} $$

Our lefthand-side is dependent on our system, so we should leave that as a path integral to keep directional information in-tact.

$$ \int_r \vect{F}(\vect{r}) \cdot d \vect{r} = \frac{1}{2} m (v-v_0)^2 = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 - E\\ $$ $$\bbox[8px,border:1px solid black]{ E = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 - \int_r \vect{F}(\vect{r}) \cdot d \vect{r}} \\ \tag{Eq. 9}$$

Eq. 9 - Our statement of energy conservation. Notice that velocity is no longer a vector quantity, as it is squared. The square of a vector corresponds to its squared magnitude, since $\vect{G}^2 is not a valid operation.

Characteristics of System Energy and $V(\vect{r})$

Let's take a second and appreciate what exactly our conservation statement is saying.

  • The sum of these quantities (kinetic and potential energies) remains constant for all $\vect{r}$, t. This is an interesting conclusion given the complexity of our quantities.
$$\bbox[8px,border:1px solid black]{ \frac{dE}{dx_j} = \frac{dE}{dt} = 0} $$
  • Vector calculus allows us to recontextualize our path integral as a scalar potential to our force field: $V( \vect{r})$
$$ \bbox[8px,border:1px solid black]{V(\vect{r}) = - \int_{r_0}^r \vect{F}(\vect{r}) \cdot d \vect{r}} \tag{Eq. 10} \\$$ $$ \bbox[8px,border:1px solid black] { \vec{\nabla} V(\vect{r}) = - \vect{F}(\vect{r}) } \tag{Eq. 11} $$

Eq. 10 & 11 - $\vect{F}$ and $d \vect{r}$ are determined by our system force and path. Our potential takes a vector input, but it outputs a scalar quantity. Applying the del operator ($\vec{\nabla}$) to $V(\vect{r})$ gives us its gradient (vector calculus).

  • E is determined by our reference frame.

Suddenly, our focus has shifted from the observable motion of our system ($\vect{r}$) to something far more abstract ($E$ and $V(\vect{r})$). Therefore, we need to motivate our reasons for exploring E and $V(\vect{r})$ any further.

  • Knowing that the sum of terms in (Eq. 9) will remain constant for all inputs is mathematically interesting, but this discovery needs to be useful if we are to introduce E and $V(\vect{r})$ as system quantities worth exploring.
