Vectorizing Newton's Equation

One-dimensional quantities like $x(t)$ are useful in cases where we have symmetries to exploit, but we need to rewrite Newton's Equation using vector calculus if we want a true 3-dimensional model of motion.

  • Encoding all of our directional information in a generalized position vector $\vect{r}(t)$ allows us to single-out elements we care about later on.
  • While this often complicates our mathematical modeling of system motion, it should provide a clearer picture of things now that we're in our own 3-dimensional space.
  • This will make vector (and matrix) formulations of quantum mechanics far more approachable.

Newtonian Formulation in Vector Notation

Calculus Operators for Vector Functions

$$ \begin{align} \vect{F} &= m \frac{dv}{dt} \\ &= m \frac{d \vect{v}}{d \vect{x}} \cancelto{v(t)}{\frac{dx}{dt}} \\ &= m \frac{d\vect{v}}{dx} \vect{v(t)} \\ &= m \frac{d\vect{v}}{dx} \frac{d ( \frac{\vect{v}^2}{2})}{d\vect{v}} \\ \vect{F} &= \frac{1}{2} m \frac{d}{dx} \left[\vect{v}^2\right] \end{align}$$
