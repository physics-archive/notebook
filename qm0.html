Understanding Position in Entry-Level Quantum Mechanics

Quantum mechanics is weird. In Schrödinger's model for energy conservation, our position function of time $x(t)$ is replaced with a mathematical object that we call the wavefunction $ \Psi(x,t)$. Why do we consider this a weird shift from Newton's Classical Mechanics?

  • In Classical Mechanics: all information about a particle system is encoded in its position function $x(t)$
  • In Schrödinger's Quantum Mechanics: information about the system is encoded instead in the wavefunction $\psi(x,t)$

Why are we shifting our focus from $x(t) \rightarrow \Psi(x,t)$? Mathematically, where is this new system-defining quantity emerging from, and how does it encode more information than the observable motion of a system?

To understand the origin of $\Psi(x,t)$, we need to first understand how information is encoded in a classical system. In Newton's Classical Mechanics, a particle's position over time - $x(t)$ - is the solution set for $x(t)$ in Newton's differential equation for motion:

$$ \bbox[8px,border:1px solid black] {F=m \ddot{x} = m \frac{d^2x}{dt^2}} \tag{Eq. 1}$$

Eq. 1 - Newton's equation of motion says that acceleration is the second time-derivative of the position vector $x$. This is a second-order linear ordinary differential equation.

Solutions to Newton's Equation (Eq. 1) leads to a deterministic understanding of the system's motion. Together with energy conservation, this leads to anything we would want to know about a system's behavior.

Schrödinger's equivalent to Newton's Equation is a complex-valued energy conservation equation:

$$ \bbox[8px,border:1px solid black]{ i \hbar \frac{\partial{\Psi(x,t)}}{\partial{t}} = - \frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{\partial^2 \Psi}{\partial x^2} + V(x,t) \Psi(x,t) } \tag{Eq. 2} $$

Eq. 2 - Schrödinger's Equation for the energy and motion of a particle. Partial differential equation whose solutions are functions of both position and time: $\Psi(x,t)$. according to Schrödinger, given by $\Psi(x,t)$. Here, $\Psi^*(x,t)$ denotes the complex conjugate of $\Psi(x,t)$.

Schrödinger's Equation (Eq. 2) is a linear second-order partial differential equation, and has the form of a wave equation. Obtaining analytical solutions to this PDE for a given system potential $V$ is almost never trivial, and sometimes it is impossible.

Where is Classical Position Encoded in Schrödinger's Equation?

Undoubtably, as bizarre as (Eq. 2) is in comparison to Newton's Equation, they must agree in the classical limits of our system. If classical mechanics can accurately predict a variety of systems, then forms to those classical solutions should appear in our quantum math models as well.

  • In other words, we need to verify that Classical Mechanics actually is an approximation for Qunantum Mechanics. We should use an easily-verifiable quantity for a test like this, like position over time $x(t)$ for a classical object (like a rigid body).

The partial differential equation for motion (Schrödinger's Equation) has solutions not of $x(t)$, but of $\Psi(x,t)$, which are related to $x(t)$ statistically as an expectation value:

$$ \bbox[8px,border:1px solid black]{< x(t) >\ = \int_{- \infty}^{+ \infty} \left[\Psi\ x\ \Psi^* \right] dx} \tag{Eq. 3}$$

Eq. 3 - Expectation value for position as a function of time according to Schrödinger, given by $\Psi(x,t)$. Here, $\Psi^*(x,t)$ denotes the complex conjugate of $\Psi(x,t)$.

In Quantum Mechanics, $ \hat{x}$ is known as an operator ($\hat{x}$ is the operator for position). Operators are how we obtain observable quantities in Quantum Mechanics, since $\Psi$ itself is not observable. This is a bizarre way of obtaining information about motion for a system; it is highly indirect in comparison to Classical Mechanics, whose underlying differential equation for motion has solutions that are directly observable.
