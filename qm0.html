Understanding Position in Entry-Level Quantum Mechanics

Quantum mechanics is weird. In Schrödinger's model for particle mechanics, our classical position function $x(t)$ is replaced with a statistical object that we call the wavefunction $ \Psi(x,t)$. Let us motivate a study into quantum mechanics by showcasing a directly-observable consequence of the wavefunction $\Psi$. Classical and quantum mechanical motion for a particle differ as such:

  • In Classical Mechanics: all information about a system is encoded in its position function $x(t)$. Solves a second-order ordinary differentail equation.
  • In Schrödinger's Quantum Mechanics: information about system energy is encoded instead in the wavefunction $\Psi(x,t)$. Solves a linear partial differential equation.

Motion in Newtonian Mechanics

To understand how $\Psi(x,t)$ provides any system information, we need to first explore how information is encoded in a classical system. In Newton's formulation, recall that a particle's position over time is the solution set $x(t)$ to the differential equation:

$$ \bbox[8px,border:1px solid black] {\vec{F}=m \ddot{x} = m \frac{d^2x}{dt^2}} \tag{Eq. 1}$$

Eq. 1 - Newton's equation of motion says that acceleration is the second time-derivative of the position vector $x$.

Solutions to (Eq. 1) provide deterministic solution set for the system's motion over all time and space. Of course, for real systems our $F$ probably does not have an analytical form for us to make such complete deductions as we can in theory.

Motion and Energy in Schrödinger's Quantum Mechanics

Schrödinger's analog to Newton's Equation is a complex-valued energy conservation equation:

$$ \bbox[8px,border:1px solid black]{ i \hbar \frac{\partial{\Psi(x,t)}}{\partial{t}} = - \frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{\partial^2 \Psi}{\partial x^2} + V(x,t) \Psi(x,t) } \tag{Eq. 2} $$

Eq. 2 - Schrödinger's Equation for the energy and motion of a particle. Partial differential equation whose solutions are functions of both position and time: $\Psi(x,t)$. $\Psi^*$ denotes the complex conjugate of $\Psi$ to account for complex-valuec wavefunctions. More on that later.

Schrödinger's Equation (Eq. 2) is a linear second-order partial differential equation, and has the form of a wave equation. Obtaining analytical solutions to this PDE for a given system potential $V$ is almost never trivial, and sometimes it is impossible.

Where is Classical Position Encoded in Schrödinger's Equation?

Undoubtably, as bizarre as (Eq. 2) is in comparison to Newton's Equation, they must agree in the classical limits of our system. If Classical Mechanics can accurately predict behaviors for a variety of systems, then those classical solutions should appear in our quantum mathematical models as well.

  • In other words, we need to verify that Classical Mechanics actually is an approximation for Qunantum Mechanics.

Schrödinger's partial differential equation for energy conservation has solutions not of $x(t)$, but of $\Psi(x,t)$, which are related to $x(t)$ statistically as an expectation value:

$$ \bbox[8px,border:1px solid black]{ \lt x(t) \gt = \int_{- \infty}^{+ \infty} \left[\Psi\ x\ \Psi^* \right]\ dx} \tag{Eq. 3}$$

Eq. 3 - Expectation value for position as a function of time according to Schrödinger, given by $\Psi(x,t)$. Here, $\Psi^*(x,t)$ denotes the complex conjugate of $\Psi(x,t)$.

In Quantum Mechanics, $ \hat{x}$ is known as an operator ($\hat{x}$ is the operator for position). Operators are how we obtain observable quantities in Quantum Mechanics, since $\Psi(x,t)$ itself is not observable.

  • This is a bizarre way of describing a system's motion; it is highly indirect in comparison to Classical Mechanics, which has solutions to its diff. eq. that are directly observable $x(t)$.
  • Here, $\lt x(t) \gt$ is the probability of finding our particle between $- \infty$ and $+ \infty$ (all of space, like $x(t)$ in classical mechanics).
