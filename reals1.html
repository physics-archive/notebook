Resolving Paradoxes in the Real Number Set $\Bbb{R}$

Consider this bizarre function proposed by Dirichlet:

$$ f(x) = \begin{cases} 1\ \ \text{if} \ x \in \Bbb{Q} \\ 0\ \ \text{if} \ x \notin \Bbb{Q} \end{cases} \tag{Eq. 1}$$

Plotting this function over the real number line $\Bbb{R}$ produces a paradoxical result that motivates a deeper study into continuity, and more generally into the fine structures of $\Bbb{Q}$ and $\Bbb{R}$ themselves.

Plotting this function over $\Bbb{R}$ is deceptive to the unexpectant observer because it appears only to violate our conditions for an ordinary function: it does not look single-valued. However, (Eq. 1) defines a clear rule that guarantees it is a valid function.

  • The irrational numbers (when $x \notin \Bbb{Q}$) are constructed from the "holes" in the rational number line above it, at $f(x)=1$. Is there a symmetry or some sort of pattern to this extreme discontinuity?
  • Problem: both sets of points consist of innfinitely-many values, even on the interval [0,1]. Once again, we must look deeper into analysis to resolve this paradox of comparing objects of infinite size.
  • This is a loose way of visualizing the property that $\Bbb{Q}$ is dense in $\Bbb{R}$

An interesting way to show that $\Bbb{Q}$ as a field is different from its subsets $\Bbb{N}$ and $\Bbb{Z}$ is to create an analogue to Dirichlet's function with one of those subsets insetead:

$$ f(x) = \begin{cases} 1\ \ \text{if} \ x \in \Bbb{Z} \\ 0\ \ \text{if} \ x \notin \Bbb{Z} \end{cases} \tag{Eq. 1}$$
